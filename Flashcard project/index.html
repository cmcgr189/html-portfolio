Year 10 Psychology Flashcards

Definition of Psychology
Is the scientific study of our thought patterns, feelings, and behaviour.
Origin of the Term "Psychology"
Derived from the Greek language: psycho which means mind and logy which means study.
Psychological Perspective
Psychological perspective refers to a particular theoretical framework or approach that involves certain assumptions about human behaviour.
Main Frameworks in Psychology
Three main frameworks: biological (your genes), social (family and friends), and psychological (thoughts).
Attitude
Our thoughts, feelings, and behaviours around an issue.
Autonomic Nervous System
The division of the nervous system that controls smooth muscle and involuntary actions.
Beneficence
There must be benefits of the research to the participants or society, and these must be greater than any risks or discomfort to the participants.
Biological Perspective
The belief that our behaviour and emotions have a physical basis (e.g. genes, hormones, etc.).
Central Nervous System
The brain and spinal cord.
Chunking
Grouping pieces of information into meaningful, larger chunks so that more information can be held in short-term memory.
Classical Conditioning
A form of learning where you make an association between two stimuli.
Cognitive Perspective
The belief that our behaviour is controlled by our thinking processes.
Conditioned Response
A specific reaction to a stimulus as a result of learning.
Conditioned Stimulus
Something that causes an organism to produce a specific response as a result of learning.
Confidentiality
Ensuring that all participant details and results are kept private and that participants remain anonymous.
Conformity
Behaving the same way as people around you.
Debriefing
The researcher must explain the true purpose of the study as well as the procedure and findings.
Deception
When it is not possible for the researcher to inform the participants of the true nature of the research.
Discrimination
Treating an individual unfairly or differently from others.
Elaborative Rehearsal
Using information and linking it with previously learned knowledge to hold it longer in short-term memory and transfer it to long-term memory.
Ethics
The moral principles that psychologists must uphold in both their research and clinical work to ensure the safety and wellbeing of their participants and clients.
Fight-or-Flight Response
The response to stressful events activated by the sympathetic nervous system.
Frontal Lobe
Part of the cerebrum that controls higher thought processes and movement.
Humanistic Theory of Personality
The idea that people are born good and that personality develops as they strive to meet their potential.
Hypothesis
An educated prediction about what the researcher thinks the results of the experiment will be.
Individuation
Looking at people as individuals rather than as members of a stereotyped group.
Informed Consent
All participants must be told about the nature of the research, what they will be expected to do, and any risks involved; they must then agree to participate.
Just-World Hypothesis
The human tendency to assume that people get what they deserve.
Justice
Participants must all be treated fairly, and access must be granted to the benefits of the research in an appropriate manner.
Learning
A change in behaviour or attitude as a result of experience.
Learning Theory of Personality
The idea that personality is a result of learned behaviour patterns.
Long-Term Memory
A mental record that can last up to a lifetime.
Maintenance Rehearsal
Repeating information over and over again to hold it longer in short-term memory.
Memory
A mental record of something you have witnessed.
Negative Punishment
Removing a pleasant consequence to discourage a behaviour.
Negative Reinforcement
Removing an unpleasant consequence to encourage a behaviour to be repeated.
Neutral Stimulus
Something that does not cause an organism to produce a specific response.
Obedience
Following orders given by an authority figure.
Observational Learning
Learning through watching people and copying their behaviour.
Occipital Lobe
Part of the cerebrum that processes visual information.
Operant Conditioning
Learning as a result of reinforcements and punishments.
Parasympathetic Nervous System
The division of the autonomic nervous system responsible for the rest-and-digest response.
Parietal Lobe
Part of the cerebrum that processes the sense of touch and integrates other incoming sensory information.
Perception
Organisation and interpretation of sensory information.
Peripheral Nervous System
The neurons throughout the body.
Personality
The relatively stable combination of characteristics that determine how a person thinks, feels, and acts.
Positive Punishment
Adding an unpleasant consequence to discourage a behaviour.
Positive Reinforcement
Adding a pleasant consequence that encourages the behaviour to be repeated.
Prejudice
An attitude that you have towards someone due to them being a member of a group.
Psychoanalytic Theory of Personality
The belief that personality develops as we strive to resolve unconscious conflicts.
Psychological Perspective
The belief that our behaviour is framed by our thinking, memory, attention, intelligence, and language.
Recategorisation
Changing our ideas about group membership to more inclusive groupings.
Rehearsal
Repeating or using information to hold it longer in short-term memory and transfer it to long-term memory.
Research Merit and Integrity
The study must be well researched, planned, run, and overseen by capable people.
Respect
The welfare of participants as well as their culture, customs, and beliefs must be protected.
Rest-and-Digest Response
The resting response in which the body resumes normal function that is activated by the parasympathetic nervous system.
Retrieval
Transferring information back out of long-term memory to short-term memory so that you can use it.
Right to Withdraw
Participants have the right to remove themselves from the study at any time without penalty.
Scientific Method
The systematic steps that must be followed when conducting any form of psychological research.
Sensation
The process by which you take in information about the world around you.
Sensory Memory
A record of all of the sensory information you are receiving at a given moment.
Short-Term Memory
Memory for all of the information you are currently accessing.
Social Categorisation
Putting people into groups based on characteristics that they have in common.
Social Facilitation
When performance on a task is improved due to the presence of others.
Social Inhibition
When performance on a task is hampered due to the presence of others.
Social Perspective
The belief that our behaviour is influenced by the people around us.
Somatic Nervous System
The division of the nervous system that controls skeletal muscles and voluntary movement.
Stereotype
Our belief about a person based on what you think are characteristics that they share with a group.
Sympathetic Nervous System
The division of the autonomic nervous system responsible for the fight-or-flight response.
Temporal Lobe
Part of the cerebrum that is responsible for perception and comprehension of sound (and language in the left hemisphere).
Trait
A characteristic.
Trait Dimension
A continuum on which personality characteristics sit.
Trait Theory of Personality
The belief that our personality is made up of a combination of traits that have a biological basis.
Unconditioned Response
A specific reaction to a stimulus that happens without learning.
Unconditioned Stimulus
Something that causes an organism to produce a specific response without learning.
Voluntary Participation
Participants must participate in the research for its own value; they must not be pressured or bribed in any way.
The brain controls everything that you do, sense, and feel.
The senses send information to the brain about what you can see, smell, hear, taste, and feel. The brain processes this information to understand it.
How does information travel from different parts of your body to your brain?
Information travels through nerves to the brain.
What role do electrical signals play in nerve function?
Electrical signals energize the nerve and keep information moving from the source to the brain.
What does the term "science" mean in psychology?
Science is a systematic enterprise that builds and organizes knowledge through testable explanations and predictions.
What is a hypothesis in psychology?
A hypothesis is a proposed explanation or prediction that can be tested through research and experimentation.
Label the following parts of the human brain: brain stem, cerebellum, cerebrum, spinal cord.
Brain stem: connects brain to spinal cord. Cerebellum: responsible for motor control and balance. Cerebrum: largest part, responsible for senses, thinking, and memory. Spinal cord: connects brain to the rest of the body.
Name three sense organs.
Eyes, ears, and skin.
What is the name for the parts of a sense organ that detect stimuli?
Receptors.
List one technique to improve memory.
Techniques could include mnemonics, repetition, or visualization.
Brain structure that connects the left and right hemispheres.
Corpus callosum.
What are gyri and sulci in the brain?
Gyri are the raised ridges on the brain, while sulci are the grooves or indentations.
Name the four lobes of the brain.
Frontal lobe, parietal lobe, temporal lobe, occipital lobe.
What is another name for cranial nerve I?
Olfactory nerve.
The white matter connecting the brain's hemispheres is called what?
Corpus callosum.
Match the internal brain structures to their descriptions: Hypothalamus, Lateral ventricles, Pineal gland, Thalamus.
• Hypothalamus: Regulates hormones and temperature.
• Lateral ventricles: Space that holds cerebral spinal fluid.
• Pineal gland: Part of the endocrine system that produces melatonin.
• Thalamus: Small circle-shaped structure located just above the brain stem.
What is the primary function of the cerebrum?
The cerebrum is responsible for the senses, thinking, and memory.
What does the cerebellum primarily control?
Motor control, balance, and coordination.
What are the three main jobs of the brain?
Processing information, regulating bodily functions, and controlling behavior.
Describe the role of the spinal cord.
The spinal cord connects the brain to the rest of the body, transmitting signals between them.
Name the system responsible for regulating stress responses and arousal.
The sympathetic nervous system.
The system responsible for the body's resting state.
The parasympathetic nervous system.
